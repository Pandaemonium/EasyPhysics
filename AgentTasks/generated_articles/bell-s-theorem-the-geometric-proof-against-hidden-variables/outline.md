# Bell's Theorem: the geometric proof against hidden variables  

---

## 1. Scope  
This article explains **Bell's Theorem** and its **geometric proof** that quantum mechanics cannot be explained by **local hidden variables**. It focuses on the structure of quantum predictions and how they violate classical assumptions about reality.

## 4. Core Intuition  
Bell’s Theorem shows that **quantum mechanics defies classical logic** about how reality works. The geometric proof uses a **triangle of possible outcomes** to show that:  
- If hidden variables exist, correlations between particles must obey a strict limit (Bell’s inequality).  
- Quantum mechanics predicts correlations that **violate** this limit, proving hidden variables cannot explain quantum behavior.

## 6. Common Misconceptions to Avoid  
- **"Bell’s Theorem proves quantum mechanics is correct."** → It only rules out specific hidden variable theories.  
- **"Quantum mechanics is spooky or magic."** → It’s lawful, but the math feels counterintuitive.  
- **"Non-locality is proven."** → Bell’s Theorem shows **local hidden variables** are impossible, not that non-locality is confirmed.

### **Section 3: Bell’s Inequality (The Correlation Limit)**  
- Derive the inequality: **|A + B| ≤ 2** (for classical correlations).  
- Explain it as a "maximum possible agreement" between particles.  

### **Section 4: Quantum Predictions vs. Classical Limits**  
- Quantum mechanics predicts **|A + B| = 2.828**, violating the classical limit.  
- Use a diagram to compare classical and quantum outcomes.  

### **Section 5: The Contradiction**  
- If hidden variables exist, quantum predictions must be wrong.  
- But experiments confirm quantum predictions → hidden variables are invalid.  

### **Section 6: Implications and Limits**  
- Bell’s Theorem doesn’t explain quantum mechanics—it rules out a class of theories.  
- It highlights the **non-local structure** of quantum reality.
