# Dispersion

## 1. Scope: What This Article Explains  
This article explains **dispersion**—the phenomenon where waves of different wavelengths (e.g., colors of light) travel at different speeds through a medium, causing them to separate. It covers:  
- Why dispersion happens (wavelength-dependent wave speed).  
- Examples: prisms, rainbows, chromatic aberration.  
- How dispersion relates to refraction and wave behavior.  

---

## 2. Out of Scope: What This Article Does *Not* Explain  
- Dispersion in quantum mechanics (e.g., photon energy-wavelength relationships).  
- Advanced mathematical treatments of dispersion relations.  
- Dispersion in non-light waves (e.g., sound, water waves) beyond basic analogies.  

---

## 3. Prerequisite Concepts  
- **Waves**: Understanding wave properties like speed, frequency, and wavelength.  
- **Refraction**: How light bends when entering a medium (Snell’s law).  
- **Electromagnetic spectrum**: Knowledge of visible light as a subset of wavelengths.  
- **Medium properties**: Basic idea that materials can slow or alter wave speeds.  

---

## 4. Core Intuition  
**Dispersion arises because different wavelengths interact differently with a medium.**  
- Shorter wavelengths (e.g., blue light) often slow down more than longer wavelengths (e.g., red light).  
- This wavelength-dependent speed causes waves to bend by different amounts, separating them spatially.  
- Think of it as a "traffic jam" for waves: shorter wavelengths get stuck in the medium more, leading to a rainbow-like spread.

## 6. Common Misconceptions to Avoid  
- ❌ "Dispersion only happens in prisms."  
  → Dispersion is a general phenomenon (e.g., in lenses, raindrops, or fiber optics).  
- ❌ "All colors bend the same way."  
  → Different wavelengths bend by different amounts due to varying refractive indices.  
- ❌ "Dispersion is caused by light splitting into colors."  
  → Dispersion *explains* why light splits into colors; it’s the underlying mechanism.

## 7. Section-by-Section Outline  
### **Section 1: What Is Dispersion?**  
- Define dispersion as wavelength-dependent wave speed.  
- Contrast with simple refraction (which assumes all wavelengths behave identically).  

### **Section 2: Why Does Dispersion Happen?**  
- Explain how materials interact with different wavelengths.  
- Introduce the concept of **refractive index** {n} as a wavelength-dependent property.  
- Use the analogy: "The medium 'slows down' some wavelengths more than others."  

### **Section 3: Examples of Dispersion**  
- **Prism**: Splitting white light into a spectrum.  
- **Rainbow**: Dispersion in water droplets.  
- **Chromatic aberration**: Color separation in lenses.  

### **Section 4: Measuring Dispersion**  
- Introduce the **dispersion formula** {dispersion relation}:  
  $ n(\lambda) = \frac{c}{v(\lambda)} $  
  (where $ n $ is refractive index, $ \lambda $ is wavelength, $ c $ is speed of light in vacuum, and $ v $ is speed in medium).  
- Explain how this leads to different angles of refraction for different wavelengths.

## 9. Optional Minimal Math  
- **Refractive index formula**:  
  $ n = \frac{c}{v} $  
  (Explain: Refractive index is the ratio of light speed in vacuum to its speed in the medium.)  
- **Dispersion relation**:  
  $ n(\lambda) = \text{constant} \times \lambda^{-k} $  
  (Explain: For many materials, refractive index decreases with increasing wavelength, leading to dispersion.)  
- **Snell’s law with dispersion**:  
  $ n_1 \sin(\theta_1) = n_2(\lambda) \sin(\theta_2) $  
  (Explain: Different wavelengths have different $ n_2 $, so angles $ \theta_2 $ vary.)
