# Energy Eigenstate

## 1. Scope
This article explains what an **energy eigenstate** is, its role in quantum mechanics, and how it connects to energy conservation and measurement. It focuses on conceptual understanding, not advanced math or historical context.

## 2. Out of Scope
- Historical development of quantum mechanics (e.g., Bohr vs. Einstein debates)
- Detailed mathematical derivations (e.g., solving the Schrödinger equation)
- Other quantum concepts like superposition or entanglement (unless briefly mentioned as contrasts)

## 3. Prerequisite Concepts
- Basic quantum mechanics: particles as waves, measurement as observation
- Classical energy conservation (e.g., a ball rolling on a hill)
- States as descriptions of a system’s condition

## 4. Core Intuition
An **energy eigenstate** is a stable, "snapshot" of a quantum system where the energy is precisely defined. Like a classical system with fixed energy (e.g., a pendulum at rest), it doesn’t change over time. When measured, it always gives the same energy value. This concept is key to understanding how quantum systems evolve and respond to observations.

## 5. Suggested Descriptive Names with Standard Terms in Braces
- **Energy snapshot {eigenstate}**
- **Stable energy state {energy eigenstate}**
- **Fixed-energy condition {energy eigenstate}**
- **Time-invariant energy state {stationary state}**

> ⚠️ Note: "Stationary state" is a standard term, but "energy snapshot" is a descriptive name to emphasize the idea of a fixed energy value.

## 6. Common Misconceptions to Avoid
- **Misconception 1**: Energy eigenstates are static, unchanging systems.  
  → *Clarification*: They are stable in energy but still have quantum fluctuations (e.g., virtual particles in vacuum energy).
- **Misconception 2**: Energy eigenstates are the only valid states.  
  → *Clarification*: Most systems exist in superpositions of eigenstates, not just single eigenstates.
- **Misconception 3**: Eigenstates only apply to isolated systems.  
  → *Clarification*: They are useful for analyzing subsystems, even in complex systems.

## 7. Section-by-Section Outline
### 7.1 What is an Energy Eigenstate?
- Define as a state with definite energy.

### 7.2 Why Energy Eigenstates Matter
- Link to energy conservation: No energy exchange with environment.

- Harmonic oscillator: Quantized energy states.
- Contrast with classical continuous energy.

### 7.5 Eigenstates vs. Other States
- Superposition: Mixing of eigenstates.
- Entanglement: Eigenstates in multi-particle systems (briefly).

## 9. Optional Minimal Math
- **Time-independent Schrödinger equation**:  
  $ \hat{H} \psi = E \psi $  
  → *Explanation*: The Hamiltonian operator $ \hat{H} $ acting on the wavefunction $ \psi $ gives energy $ E $ times $ \psi $. This defines an energy eigenstate.

- **Energy conservation**:  
  $ E = \text{constant} $  
  → *Explanation*: In an eigenstate, energy doesn’t change over time, unlike in classical systems where energy might dissipate.
