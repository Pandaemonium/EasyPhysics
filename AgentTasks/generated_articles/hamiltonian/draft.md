# Hamiltonian

## The core idea  
The **total energy framework** {Hamiltonian} is a mathematical structure that governs how energy influences motion and time evolution. In classical mechanics, it’s the sum of kinetic and potential energy, determining how systems move through **Hamilton’s equations** {Hamilton’s equations}. In quantum mechanics, it’s an **operator** {operator} that dictates how **probability maps** {wavefunction} evolve over time. While both versions use energy as a central concept, their mathematical formulations differ significantly.  

## Why this matters  
The **total energy framework** {Hamiltonian} is foundational for analyzing motion and interactions in both classical and quantum systems. It provides a unified approach to understanding how energy shapes dynamics, from planetary orbits to quantum particles.  

## The simple picture  
[Diagram idea: Energy Balance Sheet]  
In classical mechanics, the Hamiltonian is like a recipe:  
$$
H = \text{Kinetic Energy} + \text{Potential Energy}
$$  
It tells you how energy balances motion and position. In quantum mechanics, the Hamiltonian is a rulebook for **probability maps** {wavefunction}, guiding how quantum states change over time.  

### What is a probability map?  
A **probability map** {wavefunction} is a mathematical description of a quantum system’s state. It encodes the likelihood of finding a particle in a specific location, but it’s not a physical object—it’s a tool for calculating outcomes.  

### What is an operator?  
An **operator** {operator} is a rule that transforms mathematical objects. In quantum mechanics, the Hamiltonian acts on wavefunctions to predict how systems evolve.  

## The more precise picture  
### Classical Mechanics: Energy as a Rulebook  
In classical physics, the **total energy framework** {Hamiltonian} is the total energy of a system:  
$$
H = \text{Kinetic Energy} + \text{Potential Energy}
$$  
This framework determines motion through **Hamilton’s equations** {Hamilton’s equations}, which describe how position and momentum change over time. For example, a pendulum’s swing is dictated by how its kinetic energy (motion) and potential energy (height) balance.  

**Plain-language interpretation**: Imagine a pendulum as a recipe. The Hamiltonian tells you how the energy of motion (kinetic) and the energy of height (potential) combine to make the pendulum swing back and forth.  

### Quantum Mechanics: The Energy Operator  
In quantum physics, the **total energy framework** {Hamiltonian} is an **operator** {operator} that acts on **probability maps** {wavefunction} to predict time evolution. Unlike classical energy, it operates through mathematical rules rather than direct physical quantities.  

**Key distinction**: The classical Hamiltonian is a scalar value, while the quantum Hamiltonian is an operator that transforms wavefunctions. This difference reflects the fundamentally different mathematical structures of classical and quantum mechanics.  

## Limitations and clarifications  
- The "rulebook" metaphor works well for classical mechanics but is not directly applicable to quantum systems.  
- The Hamiltonian in quantum mechanics does not represent a physical "energy rulebook" but rather a mathematical operator that encodes energy-related dynamics.  
- While both versions use energy as a central concept, their formulations and interpretations diverge significantly.  

This distinction ensures clarity while preserving the Hamiltonian’s role as a unifying concept across physics domains.
