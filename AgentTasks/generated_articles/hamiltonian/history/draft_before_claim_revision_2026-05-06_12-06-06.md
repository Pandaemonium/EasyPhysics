# Hamiltonian

## The core idea  
Imagine the **total energy rulebook** {Hamiltonian} as the key to understanding how energy shapes motion and time evolution. It’s like a recipe that tells you how energy converts between motion and position in classical physics, or how quantum systems evolve over time. In classical mechanics, it’s a list of ingredients (kinetic + potential energy) and steps (how energy balances motion and position). In quantum mechanics, it’s a set of rules (operators) that tell a **probability map** {wavefunction} how to change over time. Both versions use energy as the key ingredient to shape the system’s behavior.  

## Why this matters  
The **total energy rulebook** {Hamiltonian} is the central tool for understanding how physical systems move, change, and interact. Whether you’re studying a swinging pendulum or a quantum particle, it connects forces, energy, and motion—making it essential for predicting the behavior of everything from atoms to galaxies.

### What is a probability map?  
A **probability map** {wavefunction} is a mathematical description of a quantum system’s state. It’s like a map that tells you where a particle might be found, but it’s not a physical object—it’s a tool for calculating outcomes.  

### What is an operator?  
An **operator** {operator} is a rule that tells you how to transform one mathematical object into another. In quantum mechanics, operators like the Hamiltonian act on wavefunctions to predict how systems change over time.  

## The more precise picture  
### Classical Mechanics: Energy as a Rulebook  
In classical physics, the **total energy rulebook** {Hamiltonian} is the total energy of a system:  
$$
H = \text{Kinetic Energy} + \text{Potential Energy}
$$  
This rulebook determines motion through **Hamilton’s equations** {Hamilton’s equations}, which describe how position and momentum change over time. For example, a pendulum’s swing is dictated by how its kinetic energy (motion) and potential energy (height) balance.  

**Plain-language interpretation**: Imagine a pendulum as a recipe. The Hamiltonian tells you how the energy of motion (kinetic) and the energy of height (potential) combine to make the pendulum swing back and forth.
