# Time-independent Schrödinger equation  

## The core idea  
The **Time-independent Schrödinger equation (TISE)** is a simplified version of quantum mechanics that finds "energy snapshots" of particles in systems where forces don’t change over time. It reveals how particles can only have specific, quantized energy levels—like electrons in atoms—by solving for stationary states where probabilities don’t change over time.  

## Why this matters  
This equation is the foundation for understanding atomic structure, molecular bonding, and energy quantization in quantum systems. It explains why electrons in atoms don’t spiral into the nucleus and why light is emitted in discrete packets (photons).  

## The simple picture  
Imagine a guitar string fixed at both ends. When plucked, it vibrates at specific frequencies (notes), never at random pitches. Similarly, a particle in a box (like an electron in an atom) can only occupy specific energy levels—its "quantum notes." The TISE finds these allowed energies and their corresponding "wave shapes."

## The more precise picture  
The TISE is derived by splitting the full Schrödinger equation into **space** and **time** parts using **separation of variables**. This gives:  
$$  
-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla^2 \psi + V(x)\psi = E\psi  
$$  
- **$ \psi $** is the **probability map** {wavefunction}, describing where a particle is likely to be.  
- **$ V(x) $** is the **potential energy** (e.g., a box’s walls or a spring’s force).  
- **$ E $** is the **energy level** {quantized energy}, a fixed value for each stationary state.  

Solving this equation gives **stationary states** {energy eigenstates}, where the probability of finding a particle doesn’t change over time. These states are like "energy snapshots" of a system.  

## Common misconceptions  
- ❌ **"The TISE is static."**  
  → It’s still a differential equation; it describes how the wavefunction behaves in space, not time.  
- ❌ **"Stationary states are static."**  
  → The **phase arrow** {complex phase} of the wavefunction still rotates over time, but the **probability map** {wavefunction}’s square (probability distribution) remains fixed.  
- ❌ **"The wavefunction is a physical wave."**  
  → It’s a **probability amplitude**, not a physical entity. It encodes the likelihood of finding a particle, not its actual motion.  

## How this connects to the rest of physics  
The TISE is essential for quantum mechanics and underpins the **quantum theory** of particles. It explains energy quantization in atoms, molecular bonding, and the behavior of electrons in solids (like semiconductors). Its solutions also link to **conservation laws** and **symmetry principles** in physics.  

## Recap  
- The TISE finds **stationary states** {energy eigenstates} for systems with time-independent forces.  
- It reveals **quantized energy levels** {discrete energy values}, like electrons in atoms.  
- The **probability map** {wavefunction} describes where a particle is likely to be, not its physical motion.  
- Solving the TISE requires **boundary conditions** (e.g., a particle in a box must vanish at walls).
