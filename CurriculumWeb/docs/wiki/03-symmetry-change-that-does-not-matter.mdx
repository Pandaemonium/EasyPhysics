# Symmetry: A Change That Does Not Matter

A symmetry is not just a pretty pattern. In physics, a <Term name="symmetry">symmetry</Term> is a change that leaves the rulebook unchanged.

Move a careful experiment from one table to another. If the same rule still works, the rule does not care about that shift in place.

Run the same experiment tomorrow. If the same rule still works, the rule does not care about that shift in time.

Turn the whole setup around. If the same rule still works, the rule does not care about that rotation.

<ConceptFlow current="symmetry" />

## What "Does Not Matter" Means

It does not mean nothing changes. If you move the experiment across the room, the coordinates of the equipment change. The location changes. The labels may change.

The symmetry claim is narrower and more powerful:

**The rule of change has the same form after the change.**

That is why symmetries are so useful. They tell us which differences are only differences in description, setup, or viewpoint, and which differences actually change the physics.

## Smooth Sameness

Some symmetries come in steps. A square looks the same after a quarter turn, but not after every tiny turn.

Physics often cares about <Term name="continuous-symmetry">continuous symmetries</Term>: changes that can be made by any small amount. A small shift in time. A small shift in space. A small rotation. These smooth kinds of sameness are the ones that lead to the conservation laws in the next lesson.
